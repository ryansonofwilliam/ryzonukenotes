5. Radioactivity and Nuclear Waste

Nuclear Radiation
• Describes "All particles and radiations emanating from an atomic nucleus due to radioactive decay and nuclear reactions"
• It is the change of one radioactive nuclide to another nuclide by the emission of "alpha", "beta" or "gamma" rays, or by "electron capture".
• process results in less energetic (more stable) nucleus
• Each process has a definite half-life (depending on nuclide)

Radiation and Health
• Radiation can be baad
• it can ionize atoms in the body (alter DNA)
• Large doses can cause tissue death where smaller doses can cause cancerous mutations
• Can also lead to genetic defects

Radioactive Waste
• Waste is radiactive material (inherently or through contamination) which is no longer of use
• Includes:
◇ Used Equipment;
◇ Contaminated Clothing
◇ Reactor Fuel Components
• Low waste is generated from nuclear power as there is little fuel used for large amounts of energy
• The waste generated is however very hazardous

Radioactive Waste in the UK
• The UK has waste from historic activities
• New plants (and decommissioning old ones) will add to this
• There is not currently a long term strategy for dealing with waste
• ~87% of UK waste comes from electrical generation and dealing with spent fuel
• 80,000 m3+ of nuclear waste in the UK already, awaiting long term storage strategy

Spent Fuel Storage
• Spent fuel assemblies are stored in special "Ponds" to reduce heat and reactivity
• Water in the ponds acts as a barrier against radiation and disperses heat

High Level Waste
• Mainly liquid waste generated from fuel reprocessing
• Highly radioactive and lots of heat
• 2000m3 in the UK currently generates 95% of all radioactivity
• HLW owners are required to transform waste from liquid to solid for easier containment (Over 100s/1000s of years)

Vitrification
• Waste is mixed with molten glass and placed in Steel containers
• Liquid HLW is heated to generated dry powder which is mixed with glass (pyrex)
• Molten glass poured into steel containers (400kg per container)
• 1 year of waste from a 1GW plant amounts to 5Tonne of glass

Storage
• Currently stored at Sellafield in the UK
• Projected cost for storage has increased £900m in 10 months (2012)
• Older facilities have deteriorated posing health risks to people and environment
• Most widely proposed plan for long-term storage is "Deep Geological Repositories" although none currently exist

Comparison with Coal Power Station
• 1GW Nuclear power requires mining of 50,000 tonnes of ore per year
◇ produces 5 tonnes of vitrified HLW.
• 1GW Coal power requires mining of 3,200,000 tonnes of coal per year
◇ produces 7,000,000 of Co2 and more solid waste
